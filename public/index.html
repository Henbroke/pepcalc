What is Reconstitution?

Turning the dry powder into something you can inject.

Peptides arrive as a lyophilized (freeze-dried) powder sealed in a sterile glass vial. They need to be dissolved in liquid before you can draw and inject them. That liquid is bacteriostatic water (BAC water) — sterile water with 0.9% benzyl alcohol that prevents bacterial growth for up to 28 days after opening.

The math is simple: more water = weaker solution = bigger volume per dose. Less water = stronger = smaller volume. The total peptide stays the same, only the concentration changes.

Step by Step

The mixing process, in order.

1
Plan your dilution

Decide how much BAC water to add. Common ratios: 2 mL or 3 mL per 10 mg vial. More water = easier to draw small doses but bigger volume per shot. Use the Calculator tab to preview the math before committing.

For most GLP-1s and healing peptides, 2 mL in a 10 mg vial is the sweet spot — easy math, manageable draws.
2
Sanitize everything

Wash your hands thoroughly. Wipe the rubber stoppers on both vials (peptide + BAC water) with a fresh alcohol pad. Let air dry 10-15 seconds.

3
Draw the BAC water

Using your 3 mL mixing syringe, pull back the plunger to your target volume (e.g., 2 mL of air). Insert the needle into the BAC water vial through the rubber stopper, push the air in, flip the vial upside down, then draw your exact volume.

Pushing air in first prevents a vacuum, making the water flow out smoothly.
4
Inject water into peptide vial

Insert the needle into the peptide vial. Tilt the vial slightly and let the water run slowly down the side of the glass — not directly onto the powder. Peptides are fragile; blasting water onto powder can denature them.

Never shake the vial. Never blast water directly onto the powder.
5
Dissolve gently

Set the vial down and let it sit for 1-2 minutes. Most peptides dissolve on their own. If any powder remains, swirl gently by rolling the vial between your palms. The solution should be perfectly clear when fully dissolved.

If the solution is cloudy, has visible particles, or won't dissolve — do not inject. The peptide may be degraded.
6
Label and refrigerate

Write the reconstitution date on the vial with a fine marker. Store in the fridge (36-46°F / 2-8°C). Most peptides stay stable 28-30 days reconstituted — some shorter, verify per peptide.

7
Draw your dose

Use the Calculator tab to figure out exactly how many units to draw. Wipe the vial stopper with alcohol, draw your dose into an insulin syringe, tap out air bubbles, and inject subcutaneously into fat (belly, thigh, love handles) at a 45-90° angle.

Rotate injection sites to avoid lumps and irritation. Stay at least 1 inch from your belly button.

Safety & Best Practices

Things that actually matter.

  • Always sanitize vial stoppers, hands, and injection site with fresh alcohol pads.
  • Use a new syringe for every injection. Never reuse needles — they dull fast and carry bacteria.
  • Rotate injection sites. Hitting the same spot repeatedly causes lipohypertrophy (lumps) and poor absorption.
  • Refrigerate after reconstitution. Most peptides degrade quickly at room temp. Never freeze reconstituted peptides.
  • Watch for reactions: small redness/warmth is normal; widespread rash, swelling beyond the site, or breathing difficulty = stop and seek medical care.
  • Start low, go slow. New peptide = lowest dose first. Wait 2-3 doses before titrating up.
  • Dispose in a sharps container. Never throw used needles in regular trash. Most pharmacies accept them.
  • Track everything: date opened, dose, side effects. Helps you spot patterns and adjust.

Common Pitfalls

Mistakes that ruin your peptide or your day.

  • Shaking the vial — breaks the peptide bonds. Always swirl gently.
  • Using regular sterile water instead of BAC — no preservative means bacterial contamination within hours.
  • Leaving vials at room temp for hours during a session — back in the fridge as soon as you've drawn your dose.
  • Forgetting to remove air bubbles from the insulin syringe before injecting — ruins your dose accuracy.
  • Mixing too much water — if your dose is <3 units on a 1 mL syringe, accuracy suffers. Reconstitute with less water.
